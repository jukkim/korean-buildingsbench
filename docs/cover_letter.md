**"Seven Hundred Simulations Suffice: Matching a 900,000-Building Foundation Model through Operational Diversity in Zero-Shot Load Forecasting"**

by Jeong-Uk Kim (Sangmyung University, South Korea).

**Summary of contributions**

Zero-shot building load forecasting—predicting energy use for previously unseen buildings without retraining—underpins grid-scale demand response and energy management. The prevailing approach, exemplified by BuildingsBench (NeurIPS 2023), achieves state-of-the-art accuracy (13.28% NRMSE on 955 commercial buildings) by training on 900,000 building simulations.

We demonstrate that **data quality can substitute for data quantity**: 700 EnergyPlus simulations, generated by sampling 14 building archetypes with 12-dimensional Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) to maximise operational diversity, achieve 13.11 ± 0.17% NRMSE (five-seed mean) on the identical 955-building evaluation set—matching the 900K-building SOTA within the inter-seed standard deviation. The best seed reaches 12.93%, surpassing SOTA by 0.34 percentage points.

Four controlled findings are reported:

1. **Data design dominates scale.** A matched 700-building comparison (Korean LHS vs. BuildingsBench subsampled) shows a 1.15 pp advantage with augmentation and 1.61 pp without, attributing the gap to LHS schedule design rather than normalisation or augmentation alone.

2. **RevIN is scale-dependent.** Reversible Instance Normalisation reduces NRMSE by 1.61 pp on our 700-building corpus but increases it by 0.62 pp on the full 900K corpus—a previously unreported asymmetry with practical implications for foundation model design.

3. **Rapid N-scaling saturation.** Seventy buildings (five per archetype) already match SOTA (13.28 ± 0.12%); diminishing returns set in beyond 140 buildings, suggesting that archetype coverage matters more than sample count.

4. **Sim-to-real transfer.** Zero-shot inference on 218 real Korean convenience stores yields 12.30% versus 13.14% for BuildingsBench, providing preliminary evidence of simulation-to-reality generalisation.

Together, these findings substantially lower the data barrier for zero-shot building load forecasting and offer actionable guidance on simulation budget allocation.
